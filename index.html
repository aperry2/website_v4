Ritual Video

A green screen-based collaborative video performance examining ritual and embodiment.

Collaborators: Mya Jones, Camille Jackson, CS Henderson, Raine Yung


This collaborative video performance uses green screen compositing to explore the choreography of ritual, embodiment, and the synthetic self. The performers enact gestures and liturgies over a domestic AI, which are layered over digitally constructed environments. Through this, the piece investigates how technology mediates bodily presence and sacred experience, creating a hybridized space where embodiment and ritual becomes hybridized.

This collaborative video performance enacts ritual through green-screen staging and embodied gesture, positioning the body within a technically mediated simulacrum. Drawing from Vilém Flusser’s concept of the apparatus, the camera here acts not as a neutral recorder but as a programming device that governs gestures - transforming embodied ritual into algorithmic spectacle. At the same time, Walter Benjamin’s notion of the “aura” is problematized: the performers’ presence is both amplified and dissipated by reproduction, dissolving the authenticity of ritual while opening it to critical reflexivity . As a durational loop, this work mirrors McLuhan’s idea that “the medium is the message,” embodying the time-structure of ritual in the smooth pulse of video rather than its content . By reframing corporeal ceremony through digital mediation, Ritual Video asks: how does ritual survive - or mutate - as it becomes encoded in technical imagery?

Upon a cloth of grene they dooth hir dede,
In daunce of rite and shape of bodilie,
Whil artifysed wightes serve hir neede,
And techne melteth with the seintuarie.

Ritual Unbinding of an Amazon Alexa

A live performance ritual dismantling an Amazon Alexa to critique AI servitude and techno-capitalism.

Collaborators: Emrys Brandt, Mya Jones


This live performance reimagines the deconstruction of an Amazon Alexa device as a sacred rite. By ritualistically dismantling the smart assistant, the artist critiques AI servitude, consumer convenience, and the occult logic of platform capitalism. The performance transforms labor and technological critique into a ceremonial act, asking whether liberation from techno-capitalist infrastructure requires symbolic destruction.

In a performative deconstruction of a voice assistant, this work stages the Alexa device not merely as object but as techno-relic - echoing Yuk Hui’s critique of the erasure of media origin in “the abyss of technical mediation.” Hui notes that contemporary devices often conceal their own ontological histories, and this ritual re-situates Alexa as artifact with lineage, circuits exposed and mythic voice silenced. By dismantling the device in front of an audience, it invokes Benjamin’s notion of cult value giving way to exhibition value. The dismantled artifact loses its surveillance aura but gains political resonance as spectacle and critique. Moreover, the action intervenes in Flusser’s “program”: rather than obeying the apparatus, we rupture it, reclaiming agency through symbolic destruction. In this ritual unbinding, the piece reveals how we might reclaim technological objects from their programmed roles as domestic idols.

A rite it is, this brekynge of the box,
Where Alexa’s voice is torn asunder.
With handes devout, the wight unlooseth locks,
And layeth bare the spell that draweth under.

Scrolling in the Walled Garden

A browser-based simulation that examines enclosure, repetition, and surveillance through endless scrolling.


This browser-based artwork simulates an infinite scroll, evoking the hypnotic logic of platform design and algorithmic enclosure. The piece interrogates how surveillance capitalism conditions user behavior through repetition, latency, and subtle manipulations of time. As users scroll, they encounter poetic fragments and glitches that resist legibility, drawing attention to the garden’s invisible walls.

This piece simulates infinite scrolling across a browser interface, reflecting McLuhan’s insight that "the medium is the message." Here, the act of reading becomes the medium itself: a continuous gesture that enacts the time-dynamics of platform ecosystems. It also invokes Benjamin’s concern with reception in distraction: viewers glance, scroll, skim, with the simulation foregrounding the affect of perpetual attention rather than deliberate contemplation. Drawing from Flusser’s idea of idolatrous technical images, the scrolling interface becomes a trance-inducing program that subordinates human attention to platform protocols. The work thus operates as a media-critical mirror: the user enacts their own enclosure within algorithmic horizons, and the endless scroll becomes both metaphor and method for revealing the technical mediation of perception.

The eye is drawn, but reason may nat holde,
For time is stretched, and sense doth blede away.
Such garden fair is prison shaped of gold,
Wherein our steps ben ruled both nyght and day.

Data Reliquary

Sculptural works using lab-grown crystals to to transform our Terms of Service agreements with Big Tech into relics.


A sculptural series in which digital Terms of Service documents are saved onto a USB drive and encrusted in crystals. By converting mass-consumed legal texts into fragile relics, the work invites reflection on the commodification of consent and the mythic authority of tech corporations. These reliquaries fuse organic and synthetic processes, suggesting that even data transcends humanity.

This sculptural series crystallizes Terms of Service documents - ephemera of corporate techno-governance - into lab-grown relics. By alchemically transforming legal language into mineralized form, the work responds to Benjamin’s thesis on the withering of aura in the age of mechanical reproduction, reversing the flow: mass-distributed digital ephemera are rendered singular through slow chemical accretion. The reliquary acts as a techno-object imbued with cultic aura, recalling Flusser’s notion of the “magic of the apparatus,” in which media devices enchant the user through concealed processes. However, this work de-reifies the apparatus by laying bare its ideological substrate. Yuk Hui’s theory of “cosmotechnics” is also salient: by enshrining corporate logic within a pseudo-sacred vessel, the artist makes visible the metaphysical stakes of digital governance, calling for an aesthetics of technical truth. As objects, these reliquaries resist disintegration into information - they hold, crystallize, and demand contemplation.

The termes writ in law, now closèd fast,
In crystalled shell as relique set for eyen,
Where consent’s price in brittle form is cast,
And bytes in stone like relics sanctifye’n.

Illuminated Manuscript

Projection mapping illuminates a physical book with digital light, transforming it into an animated text.


In this piece, a projection-mapped animation transforms a blank book into a digital codex. Drawing on traditions of manuscript illumination, the work combines medieval ornamentation with flickering digital motifs, suggesting a continuity between sacred texts and contemporary interfaces. It is both an homage and a questioning of the book.

By projecting animated digital light onto a physical book, this piece bridges medieval and post-digital textualities, invoking McLuhan’s observation that new media often retrieve the past in uncanny ways. The manuscript becomes a palimpsest where light and code overwrite parchment, extending Walter Benjamin’s aura through temporal layering. Unlike the reproducible digital screen, the physical book anchors the image in spatial ritual. Flusser's theory of technical images - inscriptions that detach themselves from referents - is inverted: here, projection rebinds the digital to the tangible. The piece operates within Yuk Hui’s cosmotechnics by reconfiguring reading as an embodied, spatial practice. The work resists both digital ephemerality and print permanence, asking whether our new visual technologies can recover the symbolic density of sacred texts, or merely simulate their weight.

PA book once bare now gloweth with a light,
Where script of old and flickring glyphs entwine,
And sacred text with screen is made unite
A homage payed, yet questionèd by design.

Erber Grene

A site-specific video installation projecting imagery inspired by a 14th Century poem onto church architecture.


This site-specific projection was created for a church sanctuary, mapping layered video imagery onto its altar and architectural features. The visual source material draws from Pearl, a 14th-century poem written by an unknown author. The projection investigates religious and poetic symbolism through the lens of contemporary image synthesis.

This site-specific video projection maps algorithmically generated imagery onto ecclesiastical architecture, drawing from the 14th-century alliterative poem Perle. The work reanimates pre-modern religious spatiality through computational vision, foregrounding Yuk Hui’s concept of “technodiversity”: a refusal to treat Western techno-histories as universal. Instead, the architecture becomes a vessel for spectral data, reviving Benjamin’s idea of aura not in the object but in place. The projection overlays historical sacredness with contemporary digital light, making visible the temporal palimpsest of belief systems. Flusser’s idea that technical images overwrite historical consciousness is both invoked and challenged here. The computational image doesn’t erase the past, but renders it resonant anew. McLuhan’s tetrad is also operative: projection as medium retrieves the stained-glass window, obsolesces static architecture, enhances visual literacy, and flips into architectural disintegration. The result is a mediated sacredness, both virtual and grounded.

Within the kirk where altar stones do gleam,
They cast the verse of Pearl in shifting hue,
Where symbols swim within a pixelled dream,
And pray’rs and code as one do come in view.

The Gross Gloss (Print Edition)

A print-based artist’s book using coded glyphs, vector graphics, and generative processes to obscure and reveal language and belief.


This artist’s book is a material translation of technomysticism into physical form. Using vector graphics, coded glyphs, and 3D renderings of digitla tableaux vivant, it layers medieval aesthetics with computational logic. The result is an encrypted manuscript that references both illuminated books and digital archives - an object of contradiction and beauty.

This artist’s book harnesses generative code, vector glyphs, and drawing machines to produce a tactile artifact that oscillates between encryption and revelation. Referencing Flusser’s notion of the “functionary,” the artist adopts the role of the apparatus’s operator - not to reproduce clarity, but to generate ambiguity and density. By obscuring language through symbolic systems, the book mirrors the cryptographic and initiatory nature of pre-modern codices, while simultaneously invoking Benjamin’s idea of the “secret index” of history embedded within every artifact. Yuk Hui’s framework of technodiversity is essential here: the work forgoes universal transparency for a technic grounded in arcane visual languages, challenging Western notions of legibility and utility. McLuhan’s dictum that “the medium is the message” finds resonance in the materiality of the book itself: paper and ink here are not retrograde but reclaimed, positioned as active agents in the negotiation between digital and embodied knowledge.

This book of craft with glyph and vector drawn,
Where codèd scenes and 3D forms arise,
Doth wed the monkish gold with techne's brawn -
A scroll both darke and fair before our eyes.

The Gross Gloss (Web Edition)

A hypertextual digital artwork combining dithering aesthetics, web design, and AI-generated images to invoke mystic pre-modern texts.


An experimental digital text that merges medieval marginalia with early internet aesthetics. This hyperlinked work uses dithering, vector diagrams, and non-human hallucinations to construct a glossolalia of techno-mystic symbols. Users can navigate nonlinearly through text and image, encountering fragmented beliefs, speculative code, and machinic prophecy.

As a digital counterpart to the print book, the web edition of The Gross Gloss engages hypertext, AI-generated imagery, and dithering aesthetics to simulate the opacities of mystic medieval texts. Rather than offering informational clarity, the work entangles the user in an aesthetic labyrinth of deferred meaning and visual grain. Flusser’s technical image becomes a site of deliberate miscommunication, resisting the functional transparency of the interface. Through web-based spatial navigation and recursive linkages, the piece echoes Benjamin’s idea of the flâneur - not through urban arcades, but within the networked folds of an interpretive machine. The use of AI challenges the notion of authorship, invoking Hui’s interrogation of cybernetic rationality as a force homogenizing aesthetic practice. This project stages a tension between symbolic opacity and digital reproducibility, presenting the browser window not as a portal to knowledge, but as a scrying surface haunted by textual ghosts.

Thurgh linkës and through noyse of dyverse sort,
Where margynes wroght of olde han leved trace,
Now bestës coded crepen in that port,
And sothen signes glimeren in that place.

Autoimages

A series of AI-generated images that challenge authorship and the erasure of the artist's hand in post-photographic practice.


This series of AI-generated images exposes and critiques the disappearance of the artist’s hand in post-photographic production. Prompts are displayed alongside images, revealing the underlying processes and biases of generative tools. The work questions authorship, automation, and the limits of photographic truth.

This series of AI-generated and rephotographed images interrogates the ontology of the photographic and the displacement of human authorship. In Flusserian terms, the “apparatus” here becomes doubly removed: first in the automation of image generation, then in the performative re-assertion of photographic authorship through rephotography. The work questions whether the image, once considered an imprint of the real, now functions as a simulation of intention itself. Benjamin’s concern about the mechanical reproduction of aura is radicalized - here, the aura is neither diminished nor restored, but entirely outsourced. The project also dialogues with Yuk Hui’s critique of universal computation, positing prompt-based AI imagery as a techno-cultural ritual that conceals its own metaphysical substrate. McLuhan’s observation that media extend human faculties becomes troubling: what does it mean for vision and memory to be extended by a system that has no body, no memory, and no intention?

Ful many an image, wroughte with artifys,
Ben shewèd forth with promptë set y-wryte,
Whil hand of man doth vanysshe in swich wyse -
And trouth of sight is loste in botës myght.

Technoshrine

A sculptural installation incorporating machined parts and miniature computing to produce contemporary devotional altars for the religion of technology.


A sculptural installation that combines CNC-machined components, a microcomputer, and a video screen to form a cybernetic altar. This work visualizes contemporary reverence for technological systems, drawing parallels between medieval reliquaries and our veneration of digital devices. It asks: what does it mean to worship at the altar of the machine?

Technoshrine is a sculptural installation that combines CNC-milled components and microcomputers to construct an altar-like object, evoking the sacred within the technological. This work directly engages McLuhan’s assertion that technologies become extensions of the human nervous system, reifying our desires for omnipresence and control within machinic form. The shrine, with its ritualized aura and embedded circuitry, positions computation as a locus of worship and a medium of enchantment. Drawing on Flusser’s theory of apparatuses, Technoshrine critiques the opaque technical programs that dictate our symbolic environments while simultaneously aestheticizing them. It resonates with Hui’s notion of cosmotechnics by proposing an alternative metaphysics of computing - one not grounded in efficiency or abstraction, but in spiritual materialism. Like Benjamin’s concept of the aura, the shrine maintains a unique presence in space and time, not despite its reproducibility, but because of the cultural mythos projected onto technological objects.

Ane auter newe of brasse and screen is dight,
Wher micro-mynd and engine ben y-blent;
Men worship there with eer and eye and sight,
As dede they bones of seintes y-shryned and bent.

The Brazen Head

A visual interface coded in Processing inspired by a mythic automaton said to be able to access all knowledge.


Inspired by the myth of a medieval talking automaton, this piece features a custom-coded Processing interface displayed on a CRT. Visitors interact with an archaic AI oracle, blurring the lines between myth, computation, and knowledge. The work reanimates obsolete technologies to ask enduring questions about machine wisdom.

This Processing-based interactive interface revives the medieval legend of the Brazen Head - an artificial oracle said to utter all knowledge - within a contemporary framework of computation and interactivity. In doing so, it foregrounds the continuity between alchemical mysticism and contemporary informatic rationality. McLuhan’s observation that “we shape our tools and thereafter our tools shape us” is encoded in the project’s retro-styled screen and command-line dialogue. The viewer is cast as both magician and programmer, confronting the limits of interaction with an entity that promises answers yet yields only cryptic data. Flusser’s description of the technical image as both surface and trap is embodied here: the interface simulates openness but is governed by pre-inscribed possibilities. Echoing Hui’s critique of technological determinism, the work invites reflection on the historical co-development of epistemology and interface, positing the computer as today’s divinatory device.

A CRT with charmes ful olde y-spoke,
Doth telle in code a wisdom half y-feigned;
This orisoun of wires breketh the yoke
Twixt mirour’d myth and techne’s domes y-chained.

Tracing the Materiality of a Cellular Connection

A short animation using 3D photogrammetry to depict invisible infrastructures of mobile communication.


This short 3D animation uses photogrammetry and CGI to visualize the invisible infrastructure of mobile networks. By mapping data centers, cell towers, and atmospheric distortions, the work makes tangible what is normally hidden. It is both a technical meditation and a speculative archaeology of connectivity.

This short CGI animation renders the hidden infrastructures of mobile networks using photogrammetry and 3D modeling, making visible what McLuhan might call the “invisible environment” of the media system. The piece stages a forensic archaeology of connectivity, mapping affective geographies onto algorithmic terrain. In Flusserian terms, the work reverses the function of the apparatus - not to produce communicative images, but to depict the apparatus itself as an actor within a larger symbolic ecology. Hui’s call for a renewed materialist understanding of technology is answered here: by visualizing electromagnetic flows and data shadows, the project re-inscribes spiritual presence into digital infrastructure. Benjamin’s dialectical image is realized as a network of towers, routers, and cellular nodes - each structure simultaneously a monument to global connection and a relic of spectral capital.

With art of threed and image caught in aie,
This wight y-mappeth towres and cloudës streight,
Shewing the webbe that mortals kan nat spie,
But liveth stille, like ghostë out of sleight.

The Mysticism of Home Assistants

A video synthesis project using analog tools to conjure ghostly portraits of domestic AI.


Using analog video synthesizers and vintage hardware, this project generates spectral portraits of smart home devices. Tube television apparitions flicker between form and signal, turning Alexa-like assistants into ghostly presences. The piece explores the spiritual aesthetics of domestic AI, drawing on hauntology and electronic mysticism.

Employing analog video synthesis, The Mysticism of Home Assistants conjures ghostly visualizations of AI voice assistants as uncanny domestic familiars. These nonhuman agents, marketed as convenience, are rendered spectral through visual noise, scanlines, and synthetic motion - echoing Flusser’s assertion that technical images displace meaning rather than express it. The work resists smooth, high-definition aesthetics in favor of a techno-ritualistic sensibility, revealing the latent metaphysics of these devices. McLuhan’s notion of the “acoustic space” is reinterpreted: the AI voice - once ambient and benign - is now refigured as an emanation from a haunted signal chamber. The project aligns with Hui’s argument that contemporary technology is not value-neutral but enmeshed in culturally specific ontologies. In reviving obsolete visual media to interrogate present-day AI, the piece reactivates Benjamin’s call for the politicization of art - here, through medium-specific anachronism and critical reenchantment.

The spirit of Alexa, dim and wan,
Is shewèd in the tube’s unholy gleem;
Betwene the wave and shape it fleeteth on -
An housë-haunt out of a mystik drem.

About

Alan Perry is a Chicago‑based new media artist, educator, and technomystic whose multidisciplinary practice traverses creative coding, interactive electronics, video, sculpture, and AI-augmented image-making. He holds a BFA in Photo/Image Making from Colorado State University (2013) and an MFA in Art & Technology Studies from the School of the Art Institute of Chicago (2019), where he is now a lecturer.

Perry’s work, rooted in media archaeology and speculative poetics, interrogates the ideological and affective structures of emergent technologies. Drawing from historical references ranging from wet-plate photography to illuminated manuscripts and occult iconography, his projects reveal how ritual and power persist within contemporary techno‑systems. As he recontextualizes these traditions through generative code, photogrammetry, sketch-driven AI, and CNC fabrication, he asks: how do we perform belief in the devices that surround us, and what ancestral forms continue to lodge within their circuits?
